Process of Nuclear Fusion

Fusion happens when two light nuclei (usually hydrogen isotopes like deuterium and tritium) are heated to extremely high temperatures to form a plasma. If these nuclei come close enough, the strong nuclear force binds them into a heavier nucleus (e.g. helium) and releases large amounts of energy.

D + T → He + n + Energy

Deuterium + Tritium fuse to produce Helium, a high-energy neutron, and massive energy release.

  1. Form plasma by heating fuel to 100+ million °C.
  2. Confine plasma using magnetic fields (tokamak) or inertial compression (lasers).
  3. Fusion reactions release energy and neutrons; convert kinetic energy to electricity.
Key outputs
  • Helium nucleus (inert, non-toxic)
  • High-energy neutrons (used for heat exchange)
  • Large energy release (E = mc2 principle)

Energy is mainly kinetic; suitable shielding and materials are required to handle neutron flux.

Fusion vs Fission

Fusion (The Future)
  • Combines light nuclei (e.g., D + T)
  • High temperatures; magnetic or inertial confinement
  • Minimal, short-lived radioactive waste
  • Inherently safe — no chain-reaction meltdown
Fission (The Present)
  • Splits heavy nuclei (e.g., U-235)
  • Used in current nuclear power plants
  • Produces long-lived radioactive waste
  • Risk of meltdown if safety systems fail

Current Global Advancements

Countries and international projects are making steady progress toward functional fusion power.

ITER (France) 🇫🇷

Global experimental tokamak; aims to demonstrate net energy gain ($Q > 1$) from fusion.

Private Companies 🚀

Several startups (compact tokamaks, stellarators, high-field magnets) are accelerating commercialization with novel approaches.

Research Facilities 🔬

National labs developing materials, superconducting magnets, and plasma control techniques for commercial reactors.

India's Stance & Progress

India contributes significantly to ITER and runs domestic programs such as ADITYA-U and SST-1. National institutes (e.g., IPR, BARC) and steady funding show India's intent to be a key player in fusion R&D.

  • ADITYA-U: A tokamak demonstrating high-temperature plasmas.
  • SST-1: India's superconducting tokamak for advanced plasma research.
  • Budgetary support: ongoing investments in fusion research infrastructure and international collaborations.
Why it matters for India

Fusion offers a sustainable solution for India's growing electricity demand and can secure long-term energy independence for a large and rapidly developing population.

Feasibility & Applications

Challenges
  • Extremely high temperatures (~150 million °C)
  • Plasma confinement and stability control
  • Developing neutron-resistant reactor wall materials
  • Economic scaling from demo to commercial plants
Applications
  • Large-scale, non-intermittent clean electricity
  • Desalination and freshwater production
  • Space propulsion and long-duration missions
  • Industrial process heat and green hydrogen production

Approach & Solutions

Key areas of focus to accelerate fusion adoption and ensure real-world feasibility.

Material Science

Develop neutron-resistant materials and advanced tritium-breeding blankets for reactor walls.

Advanced Magnets

High-temperature superconductors enable more compact, efficient, and higher-field confinement devices.

Project Abstract

This project explores nuclear fusion as a future energy source. It explains the fusion process, compares it with fission, summarizes global and national progress, and evaluates feasibility and applications such as desalination, space missions, and green hydrogen. The website includes an interactive calculator to estimate CO₂ savings and visual tools to communicate research to a wider audience, demonstrating the pathway to sustainable energy for India and the world.
